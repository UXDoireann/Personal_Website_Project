“…the very buildings and monuments here not stable and the greater city not stable and the wide world not stable. All alter, are altering, in every instance.”

-George Saunders, Lincoln in the Bardo

The choice of one of the columns with the composite capitals from the interior of City Hall is significant. John Summerson, the eminent architectural historian, noted how architects of the 18th century often chose that style of capital to “lay it on thick”, to express luxury, opulence and a sense of “no expense spared”. Indeed, those seen at City Hall, carved by Simon Vierpyl, with their massive volutes, ornate acanthus leaves and intricate engravings, really exemplify the intent mood of the Royal Exchange when it was originally built. Built in 1779 by Thomas Cooley, the Royal Exchange was the first large-scale Neoclassical building in Ireland, seeng standards at the time of design ambition, lavishness and style.

Dublin, occupying a significant economic role in the British Empire saw the Royal Exchange as the first of a number of superlative civic buildings in the city during the late 18th century, eg. The Newcomen Bank, the Four Courts, the Customs House among others. These grand, spectacular buildings were politically strategic gestures by Westminster and Dublin Castle to win parliamentary support and were seen as extravagant, show-off pieces to promote their interests and display power and wealth.

However, the Royal Exchange, when it was completed, in many ways, it failed as a building. Firstly, the design related to a mode of interaction and exchange typical of merchants in Europe, but the merchants in Dublin didn’t really inhabit the vast ambulatory space as intended, as their mode of dealing with each other was just different. Moreover, once the new Customs House was relocated down the Liffey in 1780, the focus area in the city for trade and commerce followed suite. Not long after, came the Act of Union which decimated Dublin economically. Capital literally left the Royal Exchange, went down the river and away. I imagine this as an over-inflated classical column that suddenly has a release of air and starts to collapse in on itself. Parallels can be drawn between history and economic collapse in contemporary times when over-confidence in building and development is subsequently dashed by unforeseen forces, with a subsequent re-evaluation of “what really matters”. “Our Capital” will also mimic the aged white marble of the statues in the interior rotunda. Instead of being completely white, there will be very subtle accents of bright colours - intended to deliberately disrupt the fixation of ‘whiteness’ in such statuary - with all its implied values.
